# Game 1 — The Two Endings: Research Foundation

*Saved from session conversation — the learning science case for the 2 best endings.*

---

## The Core Principle: Neither Ending Is a "Bad Ending"

Manu Kapur's research on "productive failure" shows players who struggle and reach a sobering ending often retain concepts **longer** than players who succeeded — because the gap between expectation and reality creates cognitive disequilibrium, which is when deep schema formation happens. So both endings need to feel meaningful, not punishing.

---

## ENDING 1: "The Conductor"
*For players who gave Claude specific, thoughtful, directional prompts throughout*

Jake submits the EP. Señora Vega listens. Tyler listens. And the verdict is: *it sounds like Jake*. Not like AI. Not like a template. Like a 17-year-old guitarist who knew exactly what he wanted and got it out of a tool that couldn't have done it without him.

**The Twist Reveal:** Jake sees his own prompts on screen — the specificity, the context he gave, the artistic direction embedded in how he asked. Then he sees what a generic prompt would have produced. The gap is enormous. He made that gap by thinking.

**The identity moment:** "The AI didn't write my music. I conducted it."

This is Gee's epistemic core — Jake doesn't just know something new, he *is* something new. He arrives at track selection as a conductor choosing which instrument to master next.

---

## ENDING 2: "The Wake-Up"
*For players who used Claude lazily — generic asks, no context, delegating their thinking*

Jake submits the EP. It's... fine. Technically competent. Señora Vega says something devastating in the quietest possible way: *"This could have been anyone."* Tyler doesn't say it was bad. He says: *"Where are you in this?"*

Jake looks at what he typed into Claude. Vague. Undirected. He handed off his thinking and got the average of the internet back.

**The Twist Reveal:** Not shame. Clarity. He sees exactly what happened and *why*. That's the moment — the understanding of the mechanism. Most people who use AI badly never see it. Jake now sees it.

Felipe appears: *"You just learned something most people spend years not learning. That's not a loss. That's the beginning."*

**The identity moment:** "I know what I need to become. And now I know the difference."

This player arrives at track selection hungry, clear-eyed, with a specific gap to fill.

## Why These Two — From the PhD Level

### 1. Kolb's Cycle (both endings are valid positions on the cycle)
- **Ending 1** closes the cycle at *active experimentation* — Jake applies, succeeds, abstracts the principle.
- **Ending 2** closes at *reflective observation* — Jake experiences the gap and reflects, which is actually the *start* of the next Kolb cycle.
Both are valid pedagogically. Neither is incomplete.

### 2. Gee's Identity Transformation
- **Ending 1** creates identity: "I am a conductor."
- **Ending 2** creates hunger: "I know what I need to become."
Both are legitimate starting identities for the tracks ahead. A player going into Track A (Fundamentals) from Ending 2 has a reason to be there that is intrinsically motivated, not just assigned.

### 3. The Twist Reveal Is the Actual Teaching Moment
Research on insight learning (Danek, Metcalfe) shows insight moments create stronger, more transferable memories than gradual accumulation. The player isn't told what good prompting is — they *see* what they did and feel the gap themselves. This happens in BOTH endings.

### 4. The Track Selection Becomes Emotionally Contextualized
Same door. Completely different sense of who you are when you walk through it:
- From **Ending 1**: "I'm choosing where to go deeper."
- From **Ending 2**: "I'm choosing where to start filling the gap I just saw."
Both create genuine motivation for the track ahead.

### 5. Productive Failure (Kapur)
Players who reach Ending 2 often learn MORE because:
- The gap between what they expected and what happened creates cognitive disequilibrium
- That disequilibrium is exactly when deep learning happens
- The "Aha" moment at the end of Ending 2 (seeing their own prompts and understanding why) is a stronger memory trace than gradual learning

## The Branching Architecture

```
BRANCH POINT 1: "The First Prompt"
├─ [A] "Write me a hit song" → PATH LAZY (2 scenes)
│                               └─ SECOND BRANCH (Señora Vega's question)
├─ [B/C] Generic+effort → PATH MIDDLE (2 scenes)
│                          └─ SECOND BRANCH (Señora Vega's question)
└─ [D] Full context, specific, personal → PATH CONDUCTOR (2 scenes)
                                          └─ BOSS BATTLE → ENDING 1

SECOND BRANCH: Señora Vega's Question
├─ "I get it now" → RECOVERY SCENE → BOSS BATTLE → ENDING 1 (learned the hard way)
└─ "I don't see it" → ENDING 2 (The Wake-Up)

Both ENDING 1 and ENDING 2 → TRACK SELECTION
```

### Key Invariants
1. Both endings respect the player — no "you failed" message, ever
2. PATH D players experience the fullest version of the game (conductor path + boss)
3. PATH A/BC players who "wake up" at Señora Vega's question still get the boss
4. Only players who truly don't get it skip the boss — and their ending is the more powerful learning experience for it
5. Both endings lead to the same door (track selection) — just with different emotional contexts of arrival
